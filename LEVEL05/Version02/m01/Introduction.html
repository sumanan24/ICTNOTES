Introduction – What is a Business?

Understanding business, business processes, and main types of businesses.

2 Content

2.1 Definition of Business

Business is any organisation or activity that provides goods or services to customers in exchange for money or other value. The aim is usually to make a profit, though some businesses (e.g. charities, social enterprises) may have other main goals.

A business is when someone or a group does something useful for other people and gets paid for it. The people who pay are called customers. The money that is left after paying for costs (like rent, materials, wages) is called profit. Most businesses want to make a profit so they can grow and keep running. Some businesses, like charities, mainly want to help people; making money is not their main goal.

What is a business?

Business gives goods or services → Customer pays → Business gets value / profit

Example: A small shop selling goods to customers

2.2 Goods vs Services

Goods are things you can see and touch. They are made or grown, then sold. For example: rice, soap, clothes, phones. You can keep them and use them later. A shop that sells vegetables is selling goods.

Services are work that someone does for you. You do not get a thing to take away; you get the result of the work. For example: a haircut, fixing a bike, teaching a class, checking your health at a clinic. The work happens at the time you ask for it; you cannot store it and use it later like goods. A barber shop or a school provides services.

Some businesses sell only goods. Some sell only services. Many sell both. For example, a mobile shop sells phones (goods) and may also repair them (service).

2.3 What is a Business Process?

A business process is a set of linked steps or activities that turn inputs (e.g. materials, information, time) into outputs (e.g. a product, a served customer, a decision). Processes help a business run in a consistent and efficient way.

A process is a clear order of steps that a business follows to do something. Inputs are what you need at the start (e.g. raw materials, customer order, time, workers). Outputs are what you get at the end (e.g. a finished product, a happy customer, a completed repair). The steps are linked because one step leads to the next; you usually cannot skip a step or do them in any order. When everyone follows the same steps every time, the work is consistent (same quality) and efficient (less waste of time and money).

Business process: Input → Process → Output

Every process turns inputs into outputs through clear steps

2.4 Examples of Simple Business Processes

Below are three examples. Each one is a process because it has a clear order of steps from start to finish.

  1. Selling in a shop: Greet customer → Show products → Take payment → Pack and hand over goods.

    The customer is the input; the sold product and the money received are the outputs. If the shop always follows these steps, selling is fast and clear for both the seller and the customer.

  2. Making a sandwich: Take order → Prepare ingredients → Assemble → Wrap → Hand over and take payment.

    The order and the ingredients are inputs; the wrapped sandwich and the payment are outputs. Doing the steps in this order avoids mistakes (e.g. wrapping before the customer pays would be wrong).

  3. Repairing a phone: Receive device → Check fault → Quote price → Do repair → Test → Return and collect payment.

    The broken phone and the customer’s request are inputs; the working phone and the payment are outputs. Checking first and giving a price before repairing helps the customer decide and avoids arguments later.

Example: How a small shop works (process flow)

Simple shop process: greet → show → pay → hand over

Main types of businesses (simple view): Businesses can be grouped by what they do. Some sell goods (shops, factories, farms). Some sell services (salons, garages, schools, clinics). Some do both (e.g. a shop that also does repairs). Knowing whether a business sells goods, services, or both helps you understand how it works and what its main process is.

3 Contractual Documents and Scope

3.1 Identify and Recall Contractual Documents and Scope Definitions

Contractual documents are written agreements between parties (e.g. your organisation and a client or supplier). They state what each side will do, by when, and under what conditions. Scope is what is included in the work or project—and what is not.

A contract is a formal agreement on paper (or in digital form). It usually includes: who is involved, what work will be done, how much will be paid, when it will be done, and what happens if something goes wrong. You need to identify these documents (find them, know where they are) and recall them (remember what they say) when planning or doing work.

Scope definition means clearly writing down what is inside the project and what is outside. For example: “We will install 10 computers and connect them to the network” is in scope. “We will not repair old printers” is out of scope. Defining scope helps avoid confusion and extra work that was never agreed.

What to recall: Names of key documents (e.g. service agreement, project charter, statement of work), what each document covers, and the agreed scope (in scope vs out of scope) so you always work within what was agreed.

Contract & scope guide your solutions

Use contract and scope to outline solutions that stay within agreed limits

Example: Contractual document – written agreement between parties

3.2 Outline Proposed Solution Range Within Defined Scope According to Organizational Standards

Once you know the contract and the scope, you must propose solutions (options or ways to do the work) that stay within that scope and follow your organisation’s rules and formats.

  • Proposed solution range = the set of options or approaches you suggest (e.g. Option A: basic setup; Option B: standard setup; Option C: full setup).
  • Within defined scope = every option must only include work that was agreed in the contract and scope. You do not add extra tasks or products that are out of scope.
  • According to organizational standards = your company has rules for how to write and present proposals (e.g. template, headings, approval steps, wording). You follow those rules so the proposal is clear, consistent, and acceptable to your organisation.

How to outline the solution range:

  1. List what the contract and scope allow.
  2. Think of 2–3 possible solutions that fit inside that scope.
  3. Write a short outline for each (what it includes, main steps, maybe cost or time).
  4. Use your organisation’s standard template or format.
  5. Get the outline checked or approved if your standards require it.

This way you explain and describe the proposed solutions clearly while staying within scope and following organisational standards.

4 Activities

4.1 Group discussion: “How a small shop works?”

In small groups, discuss the steps from when a customer enters a small shop to when they leave. List what the shop owner or staff do at each stage.

4.2 Identify process steps

Choose one simple business (e.g. a tea stall, a barber, a photocopy shop) and write the main process steps in order.
